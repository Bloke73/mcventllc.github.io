Spirit & Product Knowledge
Bar 3 written-test material — know this cold.

Fermentation

The biotransformation of organic substances via enzymes from yeast and bacteria. Yeast cells eat the sugars in your mash and excrete ethanol and CO₂.

Raw Organic Material (sugars) + Yeast & Enzymes + Water
= CO₂ (by-product) + Heat + Ethanol

Distillation

The process by which ethanol in a fermented substance is extracted and concentrated through successive heating and cooling. Many methods exist.

Distillate — the liquid product of distillation. Distillates categorized as alcohol beverages are called Spirits, separated into three parts: Heads, Hearts, and Tails.

Vodka

Colorless, odorless, tasteless neutral grain spirit from continuous stills, distilled to very high purity. Off the still it's 95–97% ethanol, then filtered through charcoal or silver and diluted to 35–50% ABV (80 proof).

Traditional mash: rye, wheat, potatoes, molasses from sugar beets. Today, any starchy or sugary plant.

Gin

Flavored neutral grain spirit, made via distillation and sometimes infusion or compounding. Flavored with juniper berries and botanicals.

London DryOriginally London, now made anywhere. No added sweeteners — juniper forward.
GeneverNotes of rye, maize, barley. Most popular in Holland & Belgium.
Old Tom"Cordial" style — masks rough notes with lemon or anise, or sweetened with licorice/sugar.
Sloe GinBritish, infused with blackthorn berries, sweetened with sugar. Purple in color.
Navy StrengthJoked to ignite near gunpowder if spilled on a naval ship. ~57% ABV.

Rum

Any distilled alcoholic beverage made from sugar cane or molasses. High sucrose content makes it ideal for alcohol production. Made worldwide, wherever sugarcane grows.

Mezcal

Made from roasting the heart of a piña (agave) plant to use as mash. Must now be made in one of nine Mexican states: Oaxaca, Durango, Guerrero, San Luis Potosí, Zacatecas, Tamaulipas, Guanajuato, Michoacán, Puebla.

Tequila

A regionally defined type of mezcal made through double distillation of 100% Blue Agave (Weber varietal). Named for the city of Tequila in Jalisco.

Can only be produced in Jalisco plus nearby municipalities: Michoacán, Nayarit & Tamaulipas.

Whisky / Whiskey

No global legal definition, but agreed to be a cereal-grain based, non-neutral grain spirit generally aged in wood barrels. Common grains: corn, barley, wheat, rye.

BourbonMust be made in the U.S., ≥51% corn (usually 5–15% barley, rest rye or wheat). Off-still ≤62.5% ABV, cut to 40–80% in bottle. Aged min. 2 years in charred new American white oak.
Canadian WhiskyDistilled in Canada. Aged in new or used American oak, min. 3 years. Bottled at 40% ABV or higher.
Irish Whiskey7 styles: Single Pot Still (triple distilled, barley malt + unmalted barley), Single Malt (triple/double distilled, malted barley), Grain Whisky (continuous still, mixed grains), Blended Whisky (a blend of the above).
Scotch WhiskyMatured in one Scottish warehouse, oak casks ≤700L, min. 3 years. Traditionally one water source + malted barley. Peat fire drying gives the smoky taste.
Japanese WhiskyYoungest whisky-making country — pioneered by Suntory & Nikka, founders trained in Scotland. More aromatic, lighter body than Scotch. Longer fermentation, ultra-clear wort, often aged in Japanese oak.

Beer & Wine Styles
Bar 2 written-test material.

Beer

LagerBeer produced from bottom-fermenting yeast, distinguished from ales (top-fermented).
Pale AleFloral, fruity, citrus-like, piney, resinous American hops. Medium-bodied, low-medium caramel, toasted maltiness.
AmberMedium-high malt character, medium-low caramel from roasted crystal malts. American-hop notes of citrus, fruit, pine balance the malt sweetness.
IPAMade with extra hops. Ranges from intensely bitter to strong citrus.
StoutWarm-fermented ale style, dark color (opaque deep brown/black), distinct roasted note.

White Wine

Pinot GrisLess aromatic than Sauvignon Blanc, drier than Chardonnay. Full-bodied, fresh, spicy, notes of tropical/stone fruit or citrus. Riper grapes = less acidic; typically medium–medium-high acid.
Sauvignon BlancBright acidity, tart apple & kiwi. Cooler climates → grassy/green flavors; warmer climates → more tropical.
ChardonnayMalolactic fermentation gives buttery notes. Big difference between oaked and unoaked.

Red Wine

Pinot NoirJuicy acidity, bright red fruits. Pale-medium color, high acidity, fine soft tannins.
GarnachaDry, semi-sweet, or sweet styles. Raspberry, black cherry, strawberry; star anise, cinnamon, black pepper if oak-aged.
Cabernet SauvignonDark fruit — blackcurrant (cassis), black cherry, blackberry — with green bell pepper, spice, tobacco, wood, vanilla.
MalbecDry, full-bodied, dark fruit nose — blackberry, red plum. Jammy, with vanilla, tobacco, dark chocolate, oak notes. Medium acid, moderate tannins.

Build, Glass & Method Rules
The logic behind every spec — use this when a recipe doesn't say the method.
  • Drinks with juices are typically shaken.
  • Drinks that are spirit forward are stirred.
  • Built drinks are made in the glass they're served in, sometimes with a quick stir to incorporate juices/spirits.
  • Coupe glasses are for drinks served up (not on ice).
  • Drinks in a rocks glass are served over ice. A guest can ask for their "up" drink in a rocks glass instead.
  • Spritz drinks are typically served in a wine glass with ice.
Mocktail rule: Mocktails are 100% alcohol-free (0% ABV). You cannot use bitters in a mocktail — bitters are normally made with a neutral grain spirit base (like heavy-proof vodka). A mocktail = no bitters. Top mocktails with a soda of any flavor as a good finishing technique.
Bar 3 Practical Note
  • The Bar 3 practical is 5 drinks in 5 minutes, and can pull from both the Bar 2 and Bar 3 recipe lists — don't drop the Bar 2 classics once you move up.
  • Egg white drinks: dry shake first (no ice), then add ice and shake again — or reverse shake — then double fine strain.
  • Specialty drinks (marked S* in the class list) won't be at every event unless requested on the client's contract/menu.
How the Styles Actually Differ
Tap a style to see what's driving its flavor — not just the description.
The big split first: yeast decides everything. Lager = bottom-fermenting, cold, clean. Ale = top-fermenting, warmer, complex. Pale Ale, Amber, IPA, Stout below are all ales — hop level vs. malt roast is what separates them.
Lager
Bottom-fermented · clean & crisp
▾
MaltHops
Ferments cold, near the bottom of the tank. That slow, cool fermentation is what scrubs out fruity/complex flavors — what's left is clean grain and a light hop bitterness. This is the "easy drinking" family.
Tastes like: Budweiser, Modelo, most "domestic" beers guests default to.
Pale Ale
Balanced hops & malt
▾
MaltHops
Moderate American hops (floral, citrusy, piney) sit on top of a light toasted-malt backbone. Neither side dominates — that balance is the whole point of the style.
Tastes like: Sierra Nevada Pale Ale — the reference example most trainers point to.
Amber
Malt-forward, caramel depth
▾
MaltHops
Same hop family as a pale ale, but roasted crystal malts get pushed up. That's what gives it the deeper color and caramel/toffee character — the hops are there to balance the malt sweetness, not lead.
Tastes like: Fat Tire, most "amber ale" or "red ale" taps.
IPA
Hops turned all the way up
▾
MaltHops
"India Pale Ale" literally means extra-hopped. This is the one style where hops are the entire identity — can swing from intensely bitter (classic West Coast) to juicy and citrus-forward (hazy/New England) depending on the hop variety used, but hops are always doing the talking.
Tastes like: Anything a guest calls "hoppy" or "bitter" — that's IPA territory.
Stout
Dark roasted malt is the star
▾
MaltHops
Warm-fermented like other ales, but the defining move is how dark the malt gets roasted — that's what produces the coffee/chocolate character and near-black color. Hops barely register here; roast level is doing all the work.
Tastes like: Guinness — the style's most recognized example worldwide.
Say it to a guest like this: "Pale ale and amber are about malt balance — amber's got more caramel depth. IPA is all hops — that's your bitterness or citrus. Stout's about how dark you roast the malt — that's the coffee, almost chocolate character."

Field Notes
Not encyclopedia entries — what to reach for and how to think on your feet. Written for you; edit freely.
"Surprise me — keep it light."
Reach for: Vodka
No flavor to fight. It's a blank canvas, so whatever else is in the glass gets to lead. Safe, fast, hard to get wrong.
Follow-up: "Any fruit or flavor you're into?" — lets you steer toward a Cosmo, Mule, or fruit-forward shaken drink.
"I like gin but every one tastes different to me."
Reach for: Gin — but ask the style
They're not wrong — the botanicals vary a lot. London Dry is juniper-forward and sharp; newer/contemporary gins lean citrus or floral.
Follow-up: "Do you want it piney and classic, or more citrusy and soft?" — this one question does most of the work for you.
"Give me something tropical."
Reach for: Rum
Sugar cane origin means it plays naturally with tropical fruit, orgeat, and citrus — that's the whole tiki category built on this one spirit.
Follow-up: "Something classic like a Mojito, or more of a rum punch?" — sets shaken-and-simple vs. build-heavy expectations.
"Something smoky, but not overwhelming."
Reach for: Mezcal (not Tequila)
The roasted-agave smoke is baked into how mezcal is made — that's the difference from tequila, which doesn't roast the piña the same way. Start light-handed; smoke reads bigger than it measures.
Follow-up: "Do you want the smoke up front, or just in the background?" — decide full-mezcal build vs. a mezcal float on a tequila base.
"Just a straightforward margarita."
Reach for: Tequila — blanco
Unaged, crisp, agave-forward — cuts through citrus cleanly without competing flavors. This is the "nothing fancy, done right" call.
Follow-up: usually none needed — this guest wants exactly what they asked for, not options.
"Something to sip slow — like what my grandpa drank."
Reach for: Bourbon, neat or Old Fashioned
Nostalgia call — they want oak, caramel, warmth, not novelty. An Old Fashioned respects that without being boring.
Follow-up: "Rocks, neat, or would you like it built into an Old Fashioned?" — respects that they may just want it straight.
"I'm celebrating — bring the good stuff."
Reach for: Scotch or Cognac, neat
Occasion-driven request. This is your upsell moment — a well-poured premium neat pour, no mixer diluting the moment.
Follow-up: "Any preference for smoky Scotch or smoother Cognac?" — then let the bottle do the talking.

Don't Overthink It

Trust your reps over hesitation. Guests want a drink made competently at pace — not perfection. Save precision-obsessing for practice, not the rail.

Memory Tricks Worth Keeping

  • The Sour "Golden Ratio": 2 : 0.75 : 0.75 — spirit : sour : sweet. Almost every classic sour (Whiskey Sour, Daiquiri, Margarita, Gimlet) is a variation on this one ratio. Once it's automatic, you can eyeball most sours you've never made before.
  • Cocktail "family tree" thinking: most classics trace back to a handful of templates — spirit+sweetener+bitters (Old Fashioned), spirit+modifier stirred (Martini/Manhattan), spirit+sour+sweet shaken (Daiquiri family), spirit+liqueur+citrus (Sidecar family). If a guest asks for something you've never heard of, ask what's in it — you can usually place it in one of these families and build it on instinct.
  • Shake vs. stir, restated simply: if it needs to look cold and slightly cloudy (juice, dairy, egg, syrup) — shake. If it should stay clear and silky (all spirits, no juice) — stir. You already have this in the Techniques tab; it's worth having memorized cold, not looked up.

What Reps Teach You That No Guide Can

  • Ticket triage: when several orders land at once, pour every spirit shot first across all glasses before doing any shaking or stirring — batch the fast step, not the slow one. This only becomes automatic after doing it under real pressure a few times.
  • Taste as you go: bottles vary — a new case of citrus, a different syrup batch — so the "same" drink can taste slightly different week to week. Reps teach you to notice that shift before a guest does.
  • Reading three words: "something strong" vs. "something easy" vs. "surprise me" — after enough shifts you start pattern-matching a guest's ask to a build almost instantly. That instinct is pure repetition; there's no shortcut to it.
  • Knowing when to slow down: speed is a byproduct of accuracy, not the other way around. Early on, prioritize correct pours over fast ones — the speed shows up on its own once the movements are muscle memory.
